Web Foundations 1

Mitigating Corporate Information Exposure on the Web

Introduction

Corporate information exposure on the web can lead to large financial and social loss for companies. Exposure can occur due to human error, malicious intent and system weaknesses. This literature review explores various journals and studies done by academics in the field of web and information security, and discusses the techniques of ensuring information security such as: employee training, risk assessments and ensuring effective vulnerability scanners are used.

Human factors

Human errors amongst employees in organisations can lead to exposure of corporate information. Safa and Maple’s (2016) report presents an examination of human errors in the field of information security, and identified the following threats: lack of awareness, social engineering and seeking information, and the following solutions: conscious care behaviour, organisational policies, knowledge-sharing and security collaboration. Whether the mistakes are intentional or unintentional, actions from employees can lead to major losses for companies, both economically and socially. Ensuring employees have awareness of the risks of information exposure, is an important factor in assuring information security, therefore managers providing training to their employees is essential to improve the user's’ awareness ( Safa & Maple, 2016 , p17).

A supporting study by Safa et al (2015) on security conscious behaviour reports that “Information security conscious care behaviour decreases the risk of information breaches when the area of weakness is human behaviour.” (Safa et al., 2015, p76). The results of their study revealed that perceived behaviour control does not affect a user's conscious behaviour a significant amount, therefore it is recommended that management inform employees of the significance of having a careful attitude, and inform them of the threats if conscious care is not executed (Safa et al., 2015).

Safa and Maple (2016) also highlighted social engineering as a threat to corporate information security. Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of individuals to make them reveal confidential information and perform certain acts. This is commonly done over the telephone or by people in disguise as workers, such as technicians (Safa & Maple, 2016, p18).

The idea of social engineering as a factor in human error is supported by Krombholzes (2015) journal on advanced social engineering attacks. As well as social and technical approaches this article also highlights the possibility of reverse social engineering, which is when an attacker makes the victim believe they are a trustworthy person. They then go on to manipulate the victim so they can access the victim's system, where they can therefore have access to the company's confidential information (Krombholz et al., 2015, p115).

Yeboah-Boateng and Boaten (2016) highlighted the problem of ‘Bring Your Own Device’ (BYOD), which is a common occurrence in organisations where employees use their own computing devices on the network of the company. They found that companies barely incorporated any security measures to mitigate the risks of information exposure, when using a BYOD system. Subsequently they recommend that management keeps a closer record of activity through the network, to ensure that carelessness from employees does not lead to company information exposure or data loss (Yeboah-Boateng & Boaten, 2016).

As well as accidental human error, Padayachee (2016) suggests that insider attacks are a large factor in corporate information exposure ( Padayachee, 2016 , p47). The Cybersecurity Watch Survey reported that 46% of respondents to the survey considered the damage caused by insider attacks more severe than external attacks (Software Engineering Institute: Carnegie Mellon University, 2012). However, a conflicting piece of research by the Boardroom Cyber Watch survey found that the percentage to be higher than originally suggested, with over 50% of participants finding insider attacks more severe (IT Governance, 2014).

The current system analysis, a review involving mapping and collating of information in a systematic manner, suggests Structure Conduct Performance (SCP) theory to catalogue information security increasing techniques. SCP theory categorises five opportunity reducing measures which are: ‘increase risks’, ‘increase effort’, ‘reduce provocation’, ‘reduce rewards’, and ‘remove excuse’ (Padayachee, 2016, p48).

A contrasting approach to insider threat mitigation uses the Routine Activity Theory (RAT) risk assessment as a method of explaining predatory crimes. The main features of RAT are inertia, visibility, value, accessibility, and absence of guardians ( Cohen & Felson, 1979). A research study applied the idea of RAT to information security systems conceptualizes and operationalizes the main constructs of RAT, and found that RAT based risk assessment and mitigation approaches are useful in the combatting of insider threats ( Jinguo, Gupta & Rao, 2015. p91-92).

System factors

Other than quantifying psychological risks for human error and attacks, risk level estimation on a network is essential to threat mitigation. Singh, Joshi, and Gaud (2016) proposed a framework for the level of risk estimation. This framework uses the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) and the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) National Vulnerability Database ( NVD) to estimate security risk level to ensure security management. ( Singh, Joshi & Gaud, 2016 , p37).

A related study by a Joshi and Singh (2016), which assessed the efficiency of web application vulnerability scanners, discusses the importance of prioritisation of estimating risk levels in web applications (Joshi, Singh, 2016. P1). Chong, Vikram, and Myers’ (2007) designed and implemented Servlet Information Flow (SIF) which is a framework for developing high-assurance web applications. Applications which employed the SIF framework are amongst the first to statically enforce strong confidentiality and integrity policies. This allowed for a larger use of information flow control. (Chong, Vikram & Myers, 2007).

However, El-Hajj et al. (2016) suggest an approach that directly interacts with the database of the web application rather than the code. SIF was built using JIF to address security issues when producing multi-tier web applications but ignores the database tier, whereas El-Hajj’s approach uses extended Program Dependence Graph (EPDG) to associate database attributes and security annotations. This framework showed a high success rate in preventing security flaws ( El-Hajj et al., 2016 , p151).

Conclusion

In conclusion human error was found to be the larger factor in corporate information exposure, and training techniques based on previous psychological research, have been shown to give awareness about how attitudes and actions have an effect on the security of company information. The importance of risk assessments was also highlighted as key, as that allows for the prevention of security breaches on a network or application. The concept of web applications employing EPDG methods was found to be more effective than the previous techniques using SIF for web application security.
